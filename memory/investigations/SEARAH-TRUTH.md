## PART I — THE SCENE THAT STARTED THIS

**April 3, 2026. RTM TV1. 8:30 PM.**

Tengku Muhammad Taufik Tengku Aziz, President and Group CEO of PETRONAS, sat in front of the camera and told Malaysia something that most people didn't fully understand.

Crude oil prices, he said, had risen 40% since late February 2026. They were now trading around USD 120 per barrel.

What he did not say on camera — but what a senior geoscientist with 13 years at PETRONAS later documented in an internal report — was the real math underneath:

The screen price of USD 120 was not what Malaysia actually paid.

Real delivered cost: **USD 140 to USD 165 per barrel equivalent.**

The difference was war risk insurance premiums, shipping disruption costs, and logistics. It was not going into anyone's pocket. It was the cost of getting oil to Malaysia in a disrupted world.

And the deeper problem — the problem that caused the energy crisis — was not the West Asia conflict.

It was that Malaysia had been producing only **350,000 barrels per day** from its own fields.

Its refineries could process **950,000 barrels per day.**

The gap had always been there. The conflict just made it impossible to ignore.

## PART II — WHAT IS SEARAH, AND WHY DOES IT EXIST

### The Company Structure

SEARAH LIMITED is a private company registered in the United Kingdom.

Not SEARA Energy Limited, as the PDF version incorrectly stated. The legal name is **SEARAH LIMITED** — Company Number **17027115** — registered at ENI House, 10 Ebury Bridge Road, London, SW1W 8PZ.

Incorporated on **11 February 2026**.

50% held by PETRONAS Carigali International Ventures Sdn Bhd.

50% held by Eni Lasmo Plc, an Italian company registered at the same ENI House address.

The board has four directors: two Italian, two Malaysian.

The company secretary is Riordan D'Abreo, whose service address is also ENI House.

**This is not a normal oil company.** It has no operating fields. Its SIC Code is 64209 — "Activities of head offices." It is a holding company. A vehicle.

### The JV Vehicle Question

The question no Malaysian has asked publicly: **Why does a joint venture involving Malaysian petroleum assets need to be registered in London?**

Not "why is it tax efficient" or "why is it commercially sensible."

The specific question: **Why does it live in English law, administered from Eni's London office, with no clear Malaysian legal jurisdiction?**

### The USD 15 Billion Question

The PDF says the deal is worth RM70 billion (USD 15 billion).

But the company's share capital is USD 2.

This is not unusual. Share capital in a JV special purpose vehicle (SPV) is typically nominal — USD 2 reflects the legal minimum. The USD 15 billion likely represents projected capital expenditure or enterprise value over the life of the project.

But here is the problem: **no JV agreement has been filed publicly.**

The actual commercial terms — how profits flow, who controls the assets, what happens to the gas when it comes out of the ground, what law governs disputes — are not in any public filing.

What we know is from the UK company registration. What we don't know is the substance.

## PART III — THE 62-YEAR PROBLEM SEARAH WAS BUILT TO ESCAPE

### The Root: Malaysia Agreement 1963

When Sabah and Sarawak joined Malaysia, the Malaysia Agreement (MA63) was ambiguous about petroleum rights.

What belonged to the federal government? What belonged to the states?

The ambiguity was a political compromise. It was supposed to be resolved later.

### 1974: PETRONAS Gets Everything

The Petroleum Development Act (PDA) 1974 gave PETRONAS control over all petroleum resources in Malaysia — including Sabah and Sarawak.

Sarawak was not fully consulted.

The promise: revenues would be shared fairly.

The problem: "fairly" was never defined.

### 2016: Sarawak Says Enough

PETROS — Petroleum Sarawak Berhad — was launched.

Sarawak's position: all petroleum in Sarawak belongs to Sarawak. PETROS should be the sole aggregator. All gas produced in Sarawak should be sold to PETROS first, at prices Sarawak sets.

PETRONAS's position: federal mandate, national interest, integrated gas market.

They have been negotiating ever since.

**62 years of unresolved dispute.**

By 2024-2025, a full gas aggregation agreement was the target. End of 2025 was the deadline.

**December 2025 came. The deal was not done.**

### The Irony Arif Identified

PETRONAS could not settle with PETROS — a Malaysian entity representing a Malaysian state — after 62 years.

But PETRONAS could, in the space of a few months, sign a USD 15 billion JV with an Italian company, register it in the UK, and commit Malaysian assets to English law jurisdiction.

The 62-year domestic negotiation that went nowhere.

The 2-month international deal that went everywhere.

## PART IV — WHY ENI, WHY UK, WHY NOW: THE REVISED THESIS

### Arif Fazil's Discovery — Revised After Verification

This section documents the thesis Arif Fazil identified, updated after UNCTAD BIT database verification on 2026-05-06.

**Original thesis (PARTIALLY INVALIDATED):**

> If PETROS wins its claim, Eni Lasmo Plc has Bilateral Investment Treaty (BIT) rights as a foreign investor. The UK structure creates BIT protections that make PETROS victory pyrrhic.

**What verification found:**

❌ Italy-Malaysia BIT — **DOES NOT EXIST.** UNCTAD database confirms no active bilateral investment treaty between Italy and Malaysia.

❌ UK-Malaysia BIT — **DOES NOT EXIST.** Previous UK-Malaysia investment treaty has lapsed. Only a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership (CSP) signed 2022 exists, with FTA negotiations still ongoing.

**What remains valid:**

✅ UK company incorporation = English law jurisdiction + ICC/LCIA London arbitration. Eni does NOT have BIT treaty protection — but they have something arguably equally powerful: **English contract law, UK Companies House regulation, and London-seated arbitration.** A dispute with Eni over SEARAH assets would be heard in London, under English law, before arbitrators familiar with international energy contracts.

✅ **Legal complexity thesis still holds.** Even without BIT treaties, a 50% foreign-owned UK entity creates jurisdictional complexity that a purely Malaysian structure does not. Malaysian courts cannot simply order the transfer of assets held by a UK company. Those assets are subject to UK law.

✅ **The structural logic remains.** The question is not whether BITs exist — it is why a UK holding company specifically. The answer is still: English law jurisdiction, ICC/LCIA arbitration, UK court enforcement. These are not trivial protections.

**Revised structural insight:**

> The UK company structure means that any future PETROS claim on Sarawak assets, if those assets are partly inside SEARAH LIMITED, must navigate English law and London arbitration — not just Malaysian administrative process. This is not BIT protection. It is **English law protection**, which in many ways is more reliable than BITs because it is backed by the full weight of the UK commercial court system.

### The Structural Problem

In a normal company, independent directors are selected by a nominations committee, approved by shareholders, and their job is to represent shareholders against management.

In PETRONAS, the government is the shareholder. The government also appoints the directors. The government also regulates the sector. The government also sets energy policy.

**The directors are independent from management. They are not independent from the shareholder.**

The shareholder is the same entity that appointed them.

He was **Chairman of 1MDB** before resigning in early 2016 — before the scandal became undeniable, before US DOJ investigations, before Najib's charges.

The question this raises is not about his personal integrity.

The question is: **what does it say about the system that a Chairman of 1MDB could be appointed Chairman of the national oil company?**

Emergency decisions. Convening the board quickly. Being present when decisions are made.

## PART VIII — THE ENERGY CRISIS CONTEXT

This section is necessary because SEARAH was announced in April 2026 — the same month Malaysia was facing an acute energy security crisis.

### What Happened

In late February 2026, conflict in West Asia escalated. The Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of the world's oil supply passes daily — became more dangerous.

Insurance premiums for ships jumped 337%. Shipping costs jumped up to 176%.

### Malaysia's Starting Position

**Before the crisis:**

- Domestic oil production: 350,000 boepd
- Refinery capacity: ~950,000 barrels per day
- Self-sufficiency rate: ~35-40%

Malaysia had been in this position for years. The gap between what it could produce and what its refineries needed was filled by imports.

The energy transition narrative — "Malaysia doesn't need oil revenue" — had been used by the government to explain lower dividends.

But the narrative and the reality were in tension: if Malaysia didn't need oil money, why wasn't it using the windfall from higher prices to build energy security?

### What SEARAH Adds to This Picture

If Malaysia was already in an energy security gap position — producing far less than it refines — the question is whether SEARAH helps close that gap or widens it.

The geoscientist's assessment: **the gap cannot be closed quickly.** New field development takes 5-8 years. Deepwater drilling in Sabah requires specialized rigs at USD 500,000-1 million per day.

The 45-day planning horizon is not a strategy. It is an acknowledgment that Malaysia has almost no buffer.

SEARAH is a long-term capital commitment. It does not address the short-term supply gap.
