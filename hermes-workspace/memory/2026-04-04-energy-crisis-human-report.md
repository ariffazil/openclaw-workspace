# PART I: WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED

## The Short Version

Something serious is happening to Malaysia's energy supply. It started in late February 2026 when conflict broke out in West Asia — the same region that sits right next to the Strait of Hormuz, which is basically the world's most important oil and gas highway.

About 20% of the world's oil supply passes through that strait every single day. When things get disrupted there, the whole world feels it.

But here's what's really worrying: Malaysia was already in trouble before this happened. Our own oil and gas production had fallen to its lowest level in years. And the people in charge are not being completely honest about how bad it is.

This report is my attempt to tell you what's actually going on — using real numbers, real geology, and real logic. Not political spin.

## The Oil Production Crisis — It's Worse Than They Say

Here's the number that matters most:

**Malaysia's total domestic oil and gas production: 350,000 barrels per day (boepd)**

That sounds like a lot. It's not.

Here's why. Malaysia has about 34 million people. Our refineries can process nearly 1 million barrels of crude per day. But our own fields only produce 350,000 barrels per day.

That means we can only supply about 35-40% of what our refineries need. The rest has to come from overseas.

And here's the thing nobody is saying out loud: this 350,000 boepd figure — that's total Malaysia, from Peninsular, Sarawak and Sabah combined. It's not getting better. It's getting worse every year as our old fields run dry.

**What I've seen in my career:** The Tapis field in the Peninsular — that's one of our most important — has been in decline for years. Water is flooding through the reservoirs, which means less oil comes out. The fields that were easy to produce from in the 1990s and 2000s are now mostly depleted.

The deepwater fields in Sabah — there are some promising ones, but it takes 5 to 8 years from finding oil to actually pumping it out commercially. We can't drill our way out of this crisis in 45 days.

The crude oil it processes isn't Malaysian crude. It's Saudi crude. And right now, getting that crude to Malaysia is much harder and more expensive because of the Hormuz situation.

So when PETRONAS says they have "50% market share" of Malaysia's fuel supply, that's technically true. But the other 50% isn't really a competitor — it's the import dependency we've been hiding behind a good refinery name.

**The real situation:** Our own refineries can only run at about 35-40% capacity using our own crude. The rest needs imported oil, which is now more expensive and more dangerous to transport.

## The Price Reality — What the CEO Said vs. What He Meant

PETRONAS CEO Tengku Muhammad Taufik was on TV on April 3 and said crude oil prices have risen 40% since late February 2026, now trading around US$120 per barrel.

That's true. But he also explained something most people missed: the US$120 you see on the screen isn't what Malaysia actually pays.

Here's why:

**The actual cost of delivered oil:**
```
Screen price:              US$120 per barrel
+ Shipping costs:          up to 176% higher than before
+ Insurance:               up to 337% higher (war risk premiums)
+ Logistics in Malaysia:   port, pipeline, refinery fees
= REAL delivered cost:     US$140 to US$165 per barrel equivalent
```

The difference between the screen price and what Malaysia actually pays is US$20 to US$45 per barrel. That's the "hidden tax" from this crisis. It's not going into anyone's pocket — it's just the cost of getting oil here safely.

For context: the UK's FTSE and US stock markets have both fallen recently partly because investors are worried about energy costs. Malaysia is not immune from this.

## The Gas Situation — The Quiet Emergency

This is the part nobody is talking about, but it's actually more worrying than the oil situation.

Here's what the PETRONAS CEO mentioned briefly: more than 30% of the world's fertilizer supply — urea, ammonia, potash, phosphate — goes through the Strait of Hormuz.

Why does fertilizer matter? Because it grows food. Palm oil, rice, vegetables, chicken feed — they all need fertilizer. If fertilizer prices spike, food prices spike.

But let me focus on the gas side first, because that's more immediate.

**How gas gets to Peninsular Malaysia:**
1. Gas from domestic fields in the Peninsular (depleting fast)
2. Gas from a pipeline from Thailand (they're already helping us — we started importing from them recently)
3. LNG from the Bintulu plant in Sarawak (but Sarawak gas is already sold to Japan and Korea under long-term contracts)

The pipeline from Thailand? We're grateful for it. But Thailand is also dealing with their own energy situation. They can't save us.

The Sarawak gas? It's contractually committed to Japan and Korea. Malaysia can't just redirect it to the Peninsular when we have a shortage — that would mean breaking international contracts and paying massive penalties.

**The worst case scenario — and I'm not saying this will happen, but it's worth thinking about:** If LNG prices spike so high that some countries start cancelling or renegotiating contracts, companies like PETRONAS might declare "force majeure" — which basically means "we can't fulfil our contracts right now due to circumstances beyond our control." If that happens, Japan and Korea get less gas, and Malaysia's international reputation takes a hit.

There was a previous case where Malaysia's MLNG plant had to declare force majeure. It was embarrassing. The CEO of the time got hauled in front of Parliament. Nobody wants that to happen again.

**Why the shipping cost matters for gas:** A lot of LNG comes on special ships. Malaysia's shipping company MISC operates some of these vessels. If oil shipping costs jump 47-176% and insurance jumps 337%, that affects everything transported by sea — not just crude oil.

# PART III: THE FOOD CONNECTION — THE CRISIS YOU'RE NOT SEEING YET

## Why Fertilizer Is the Real Hidden Crisis

I want to be very clear about something the PETRONAS CEO mentioned that most news reports completely missed:

**He said more than 30% of global fertilizer supply — urea, ammonia, potash, and phosphate — moves through the Strait of Hormuz.**

Let me explain what this means for you:

**Palm oil:** Malaysia is the world's second largest producer. Palm oil is in everything — cooking oil, instant noodles, soap, cosmetics. It sounds simple, but palm oil plantations use a LOT of fertilizer. If fertilizer prices go up 50%, the cost of growing palm oil goes up, and eventually so does the price of your cooking oil and everything made with it.

**Rice:** Malaysia produces about 65-70% of the rice we eat. The rest we import, mainly from Thailand and Vietnam. But here's the scary part: Thailand and Vietnam ALSO use fertilizer, and they ALSO rely partly on supplies that transit Hormuz. If their fertilizer costs spike, rice prices go up for everyone in Southeast Asia.

**Chicken and meat:** Animals eat grain — corn, soy, wheat byproducts. Grain prices are tied to fertilizer costs. Expensive fertilizer → expensive feed → more expensive chicken, eggs, and beef.

**The cascade no one is talking about:**
```
Hormuz disruption
→ Less fertilizer on the world market
→ Fertilizer prices jump 40-60%
→ Palm oil farmers cut fertilizer use
→ Palm oil yields drop
→ Cooking oil prices spike in 6-12 months
→ Rice farmers also affected
→ Rice prices spike
→ Instant noodles, bread, chicken feed all more expensive
→ Cost of living jumps not immediately but in 6-18 months
```

This is not fear-mongering. This is exactly how the 2008 food price spike happened, and it led to food riots in more than 30 countries. We're starting from a similar place in 2026.

## Why Southeast Asia Is More Vulnerable Than Europe

People compare this to the 1970s oil crisis. That's fair in some ways. But here's the thing: Europe and America have money. They can pay more for energy. They have strategic reserves. They have better safety nets.

Southeast Asia has less of all of those things.

Think about it country by country:

**Indonesia:** Actually okay. They produce their own coal, oil, and gas. They might even profit from this as an energy exporter.

**Thailand:** Slightly better than Malaysia. Less dependent on oil imports. But not immune.

**Singapore:** Very exposed — 100% dependent on imported energy. But they're rich and have strategic reserves. They'll survive, but it'll cost them.

**Vietnam:** Coal-heavy. Less immediately exposed to oil/gas crisis. More exposed to coal price volatility.

**Philippines:** Very exposed. Poorest in the group. High energy costs = more poverty.

**Malaysia:** Somewhere in the middle — bad position, not enough resources, but also not the poorest. The worst positioned are the people at the bottom of the income scale who spend 40-50% of their income on food and energy.

**What they say:** "We're cutting our dependency on oil revenue"

**What they don't say:** This narrative was always partly true and partly convenient politics. Now that energy prices have spiked, Malaysia can't benefit from higher oil prices the way an oil producer should — because they've already committed to the narrative that "we don't need oil revenue."

The Prime Minister proudly announced the government will receive "only RM20 billion" in PETRONAS dividends this year, framing it as wise fiscal management. But here's the problem: RM20 billion is a lot less than the RM30 to 40 billion we used to receive. And the reason given — "we're reducing our dependence on oil" — only works if you ALSO have energy security. We don't. We have neither the money NOR the supply.

This is what I would call a "policy trap." You can't simultaneously say "we don't need oil money" AND face an oil supply crisis AND not have alternatives ready. All three things are true at once and they're contradictory.

# PART V: WHAT A GEOLOGIST ACTUALLY SEES

## Why This Problem Can't Be Fixed Quickly

I want to explain why the "45-day planning horizon" or "alternative sourcing" approaches are not real solutions. Because they can't be.

**Why we can't just find new oil quickly:**
- Finding new oil: 3 to 7 years of exploration, drilling, appraisal
- Even if we found something tomorrow: 5 to 8 years to get it to production
- Malaysia's mature fields (Tapis, Bekok, Palas in the Peninsular): these are Jurassic to Miocene age deltaic sandstone reservoirs that have been producing for 40 to 60 years. They're in decline. Water is flooding through them. No amount of money can reverse that geology.
- The deepwater options in Sabah: yes, there are prospects. But they're in 1,500 to 3,000 meters of water. That requires specialized drilling rigs that cost US$500,000 to US$1 million per day. That's before you even find anything.

**Why we can't just switch to gas quickly:**
- We don't have enough domestic gas
- Sarawak gas is committed to Japan and Korea
- Building new gas infrastructure takes 3 to 10 years
- The LNG import terminals we have take time to expand

**Why we can't just use renewable energy:**
- Solar and wind are great for electricity
- But our cars, trucks, ships, and planes still run on oil
- Our factories still need oil-based inputs
- The energy transition is real but it's a 30 to 50 year project, not a 5 year one
- You can't replace 350,000 boepd with solar panels in 5 years. It's physically impossible.

This is the uncomfortable truth that nobody in government wants to say out loud: **we spent years being told the energy transition would save us, and it won't. Not in time to help with this crisis.**

# PART VI: SCENARIOS — WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS NEXT

## The Four Most Likely Outcomes

**Scenario 1: Things Clear Up By May (30% chance)**
The conflict in West Asia de-escalates. Shipping lanes partially reopen. Insurance premiums drop by half. Oil prices fall back to US$85-95 per barrel by mid-year. PETRONAS's "45-day plan" turns out to have been an overreaction. Life goes on. Everyone breathes.

This is the scenario I hope for. But I assign it only about 30% probability because there's no ceasefire signal yet.

**Scenario 2: Stalemate Through End of 2026 (25% chance)**
Low-intensity conflict continues. Insurance premiums stay high. Oil stays at US$100-120. Malaysia's supply stays tight. Government activates demand management measures — encouraging people to reduce fuel consumption. No rationing, but noticeably higher prices at the pump for 6 to 12 months. This is manageable but painful.

**Scenario 3: Things Get Worse — Hormuz Seriously Disrupted (10% chance)**
The conflict escalates. Ships are attacked or refuse to transit. Qatar LNG contracts are voided or renegotiated. Malaysia has to declare energy emergency. Rationing begins — perhaps odd-even plate system for fuel, or priority for essential services. This is the bad scenario.

**Scenario 4: Worst Case — Full Supply Chain Collapse (3% chance)**
Multiple things go wrong at once: Hormuz closes, South China Sea tensions spike, Taiwan situation worsens. Global trade disrupted. Oil hits US$150-180 per barrel. LNG becomes unaffordable for many. Food price spike becomes a food crisis. This is the scenario that sounds like fear-mongering but has actually happened in previous energy crises. It would require a full national response.

I put this at only 3% but I want you to know it exists.

# PART VII: WHAT SHOULD ACTUALLY BE DONE

## Five Things That Would Actually Help

I'm going to be direct here. These are not easy. Some of them cost money. Some take years. But these are the real answers, not the political ones.

**1. Tell people the truth**
The government should hold a proper press conference — not a political statement, but an actual technical briefing — explaining Malaysia's energy situation honestly. Include the 350,000 boepd figure. Include the refinery utilization rate. Include the realistic timeline for new discoveries. Adults can handle the truth.

**2. Activate energy rationing protocols now, not later**
If we're heading into a potential supply crunch, the time to prepare is now, not when the tanks are half empty. A modest fuel rationing program — not punitive, just smart — would extend our buffer. This could be as simple as giving government offices a fuel allowance, or encouraging businesses to stagger work hours to reduce peak demand.

**3. Renegotiate the Pengerang joint venture**
The biggest refinery in Malaysia is a joint venture with Saudi Aramco. Saudi Arabia has its own interests. They want to sell oil, not solve Malaysia's domestic supply problem. We need to renegotiate this so that Malaysia gets first call on a portion of that refinery's output for domestic use, at domestic prices, not world market prices. This will be uncomfortable diplomatically. It must happen.

**4. Immediately begin strategic petroleum reserve**
Malaysia has no strategic oil reserve. That's like having no savings account. We should charter an offshore floating storage vessel immediately — it's faster than building a cavern — and start storing 30 days of crude. It costs maybe US$1-1.5 billion. It would pay for itself the first time a real crisis hits.

**5. Accelerate Sabah deepwater exploration — award new blocks this year**
The only place in Malaysia that has meaningful potential for new oil and gas discoveries is Sabah's deepwater. It's not guaranteed — exploration is always a gamble — but the geological chance of success is real. The government should immediately offer new production sharing contracts to companies willing to drill. Yes, it's expensive. Yes, it takes 5 to 8 years. But you have to start somewhere.
