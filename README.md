## Who I Am

My name is ARIF FAZIL. I am a Senior Exploration Geoscientist at PETRONAS Carigali, based in Malaysia, with over thirteen years of work that has taken me deep into the offshore basins of Sabah and Peninsular Malaysia. I trained as a PETRONAS Scholar, earned a double major in Geology & Geophysics and Economics from the University of Wisconsin-Madison, and have spent most of my career doing one thing: listening to the Earth and trying to understand what it is saying.

I am not a fast-mover. I am a grinder. I believe in the slow accumulation of evidence, the careful testing of assumptions, and the discipline of not guessing when the data has more to say. That discipline — the one I learned drilling into sedimentary basins — is the same one I now apply to artificial intelligence.

This repository is the **human layer** of the arifOS ecosystem. It is where I keep my personal site, my professional history, and the context that explains why I am building what I am building.

## What I've Built Underground

Exploration geoscience is a strange profession. You spend months — sometimes years — building a case for something you cannot see. You gather seismic lines, core samples, pressure data, and analogues. You build a model. Then you drill, and the Earth either confirms you or corrects you. Both outcomes teach you something.

Here are some of the wells I have been part of, each one a real point of contact between a hypothesis and the truth.

---

### BEKANTAN-1

The Bekantan-1 well is one I think about often. It was drilled in what many considered a mature area of the Sabah Basin — a place where most people assumed the easy finds had already been made. The prevailing assumption was that there was nothing left worth looking for in shallow structures.

We disagreed.

The well found hydrocarbons. It reminded everyone in the room that "mature basin" is not the same as "exhausted basin," and that fresh eyes applied to old data can still find things that were missed. For me, Bekantan-1 stands for a principle I carry into everything I do: do not inherit other people's conclusions.

---

### PUTERI BASEMENT-1

This one was fundamentally structural. The Puteri Basement-1 well targeted fractured basement rock — a harder play to mature, with more uncertainty about the reservoir itself. What made it work was returning to basic structural analysis, tracing the fault geometry carefully, and not letting the complexity of the system obscure the underlying logic.

The well succeeded. It is a reminder that the most fundamental techniques, applied rigorously, still produce results that sophisticated models sometimes miss. I learned more about patience and structural thinking from Puteri Basement-1 than from any training course I have attended.

---

### LEBAH EMAS-1

Lebah Emas-1 was a faulting problem. The architecture of the trap depended entirely on understanding how the fault system behaved — where it sealed, where it leaked, how far the compartmentalization extended. Getting the faulting interpretation wrong would have meant drilling an empty structure. Getting it right meant finding something real.

We got it right. The well gave me a deep respect for the geometry of failure — how the ways a system breaks are often the keys to where it holds.

---

### BUNGA TASBIH-1

BUNGA TASBIH-1 is part of the Malay Basin story, and it is one of those wells that represents a particular kind of quiet victory. Located in the shallow offshore waters of Peninsular Malaysia, it contributed to a portfolio of Discovered Resource Opportunities that PETRONAS later marketed through the Malaysia Bid Round Plus (MBR+) programme. In July 2024, the Bunga Tasbih field — alongside Bubu and Enau — was awarded under a Small Field Asset cluster Production Sharing Contract to Ping Petroleum and Duta Marine.

What I value about BUNGA TASBIH-1 is what it represents in terms of basin maturity: the Malay Basin has been explored for over fifty years, and there are still fields worth finding and developing. You just have to look more carefully, think more clearly, and be willing to work a smaller prize with the same rigour you would bring to a giant. It is the kind of work that does not make headlines, but it is the backbone of a national energy programme.

## The Work Outside the Basin

In 2024, I started building something that had been forming in my head for years.

I called it **arifOS**.

The idea began simply: the same principles I use to evaluate subsurface systems — evidence hierarchies, reversibility, uncertainty quantification, the discipline of not claiming more than the data supports — should apply to artificial intelligence systems. AI systems are making consequential decisions. They should be governed by law, not just optimised by loss functions.

arifOS is a constitutional governance kernel for AI. It is not a product. It is a framework — a set of laws that define what an AI system is allowed to do, how it must document what it has done, and what happens when it fails. I built it in the same spirit I bring to drilling: start with what you know, be honest about what you don't, and build something that will survive the test of contact with reality.
