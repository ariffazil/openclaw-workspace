n the morning of November 3, 2025, in a conference room at the Abu Dhabi National Exhibition Centre, two men signed a piece of paper that would reshape who controls Malaysia's petroleum wealth. One was Claudio Descalzi, chief executive of Italy's state-controlled energy company Eni SpA, a man who had just been appointed to an unprecedented fifth consecutive term leading one of Europe's largest energy companies. The other was Tengku Muhammad Taufik, president and group chief executive of PETRONAS. They called the document an Investment Agreement. It committed more than $15 billion. It created a new company. And it placed some of Malaysia's most valuable petroleum assets under the laws of England and Wales.

Most Malaysians heard nothing about it that day. Parliament was not in session. The newspapers that reported the signing described it as a routine joint venture. The word SEARAH — the name chosen for the new company — appeared in press releases from Milan and Kuala Lumpur. It was noted by energy analysts. It was noted by competitors. It was noted by lawyers who specialise in international investment law. It was not explained to the people of Sarawak, whose offshore gas fields are central to the deal.

Fifteen weeks later, on March 16, 2026, Malaysia's Federal Court agreed to hear a landmark challenge brought by PETRONAS against the laws of Sarawak — a challenge that could determine whether PETRONAS or the Sarawak state government controls the gas fields that feed the Bintulu LNG complex. The court case had been building for months. The settlement negotiations had failed. The politicians had stepped back. And in the weeks while all of that was happening, a company had already been registered in London, with two Italian directors and two Malaysian directors, whose entire purpose was to hold the very assets now being disputed.

"A country can lose control of its resources not through theft alone, but through structure. Through jurisdictions chosen deliberately. Through decisions made in places the rakyat cannot reach."

— From This Investigation

This is not a story about crime. No one has been charged with any wrongdoing. This is a story about structure, about sovereignty, about the distance that can open up between a national resource and the people it belongs to — when the wrong legal architecture is placed in between.

The Deal
What Was Signed in Abu Dhabi

The Investment Agreement dated November 3, 2025, committed PETRONAS and Eni to a 50/50 joint venture covering upstream petroleum assets across Malaysia and Indonesia. Eni described it as a "transformational" combination that would create a major new independent energy company. PETRONAS described it as a partnership that would generate "substantial synergies" and position both companies as leading LNG players in Southeast Asia. Both descriptions were accurate. Neither mentioned the most important thing about the structure.

The new company, registered in the United Kingdom under the Companies Act 2006, is called SEARAH Limited. It was incorporated at Companies House on February 11, 2026, under the name SEARA ENERGY LIMITED — a name it changed on March 30, 2026, twenty-two days after the Malaysian Federal Court agreed to hear the PETRONAS-PETROS case. Its registered address is ENI House, 10 Ebury Bridge Road, London SW1W 8PZ. Its two shareholders are Petronas Carigali International Ventures Sdn. Bhd. and Eni Lasmo Plc, a UK-registered Eni subsidiary. Its board has two Italian directors and two Malaysian directors. Its company secretary is Eni House. It operates under English law.

The Numbers Are Large

SEARAH's asset portfolio consists of 19 fields across Malaysia and Indonesia. Five are in Malaysia. Fourteen are in Indonesia. The Malaysian assets are gas-producing and development-stage fields, including fields in PETRONAS's largest domestic producing block — Block SK316, located offshore Sarawak, approximately 200 kilometres off the coast, containing the Kasawari field which began production in August 2024 and supplies feedgas to the MLNG complex at Bintulu. The Indonesian assets include Eni's holdings in the Kutei Basin and the Masela Block containing the Abadi LNG project. Initial production is rated above 300,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day, scaling to more than 500,000 boe/day within five years. The committed capital investment exceeds $15 billion over that period. The reserves base is approximately 3 billion barrels of oil equivalent, with a further 10 billion boe of exploration upside.

Both CEOs Signed at ADIPEC

Both CEOs signed the agreement in Abu Dhabi on November 3, 2025, at ADIPEC. Descalzi described the venture as "a transformational model for Eni's Southeast Asian presence," enabled by "our exceptional exploration skills and track record of delivering disciplined, fast track projects." No PETRONAS statement described the UK structure as having been recommended by Malaysian legal counsel or reviewed by the Federal Cabinet. No press release mentioned that the assets included fields in Sarawak whose legal status was then actively in dispute.

The Sequence of Events

The Timing Is Difficult to Ignore

The sequence of events runs as follows. In August 2025, Petroleum Sarawak Berhad — known as PETROS, the 100%-state-owned petroleum company of Sarawak — filed a lawsuit against PETRONAS in the Kuching High Court, alleging that PETRONAS had been supplying natural gas in Sarawak without a valid license. In October 2025, Eni publicly confirmed that the joint venture was targeting a 2026 launch. On October 29, 2025, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim said he had spoken to Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni and both had agreed to "expedite and conclude" the PETRONAS-Eni collaboration "in the near future." On November 3, 2025, the Investment Agreement was signed in Abu Dhabi. SEARA ENERGY LIMITED was incorporated in London on February 11, 2026. On February 25, 2026, the Kuching High Court ruled on the bank guarantee question in the PETROS-PETRONAS dispute, sending the core jurisdictional question to the Federal Court. On March 16, 2026, the Federal Court agreed to hear the case. On March 30, 2026 — fourteen days after the Federal Court ruling — SEARA ENERGY LIMITED changed its name to SEARAH LIMITED. The name change is a matter of public record at Companies House.

The gas from Kasawari and the other fields in Block SK316 flows to Bintulu. Bintulu is in Sarawak. PETROS was created specifically to be the gas distribution authority for Sarawak. The PETROS lawsuit is specifically about whether PETRONAS has been authorised to supply that gas. And now, the asset that produces that gas is held — in part — inside a UK company whose governance is controlled by English law, whose board has a 50% Italian shareholder, and whose corporate actions cannot be directly overridden by Malaysian courts without navigating UK company law — creating significant jurisdictional complexity for any state action related to those assets.

The Field
Why Sarawak's Gas Is Central to This Deal

Block SK316 is PETRONAS's largest domestic producing asset. That is not an opinion — it is the finding of Wood Mackenzie, one of the world's most respected energy research firms, in a February 2025 report written specifically about the PETRONAS-Eni combination. "PETRONAS's largest domestic producing asset is the SK316 block in Sarawak," the report states, "which includes the Kasawari development." Kasawari is a gas field located in the Sarawak Basin, in the Central Luconia Province, in approximately 100 metres of water depth, about 200 kilometres off the coast of Sarawak. It began producing gas in August 2024 at an initial rate of 200 million standard cubic feet per day, with a design capacity of over 9,300 million cubic metres per year. It is expanding toward 900 mmscf/d. Its production feeds directly into the MLNG Train 9 facility at Bintulu, Sarawak — the same facility at the centre of the PETROS-PETRONAS dispute.

Block SK316 is not disputed in the abstract. It is disputed in law. PETROS's core legal argument is that PETRONAS has been supplying gas in Sarawak without the required authorisation from the state — because, PETROS argues, the Petroleum Development Act 1974 does not validly apply in Sarawak, having been passed during the Emergency rule when the federal government had limited legitimate authority in the states.

The gas from Kasawari and the other fields in Block SK316 flows to Bintulu. The PETROS lawsuit is about whether PETRONAS has been authorised to supply that gas. SEARAH's corporate structure now holds — in part — the very assets at the centre of that dispute.

The MLNG complex at Bintulu is one of the largest liquefied natural gas export facilities in the world. It supplies gas to Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China under long-term contracts. Those contracts are not adjustable in the short term. If the dispute disrupts supply, the consequences are not abstract.

The Architect
Claudio Descalzi and the SEARAH Structure

Claudio Descalzi has been the chief executive of Eni SpA since May 2014. He was born in Milan on February 27, 1955, trained as an engineer, and has spent his entire career at Eni, rising through the exploration and production division before becoming chief executive. In April 2026, the Italian Treasury confirmed his appointment to an unprecedented fifth consecutive three-year term — a remarkable tenure for a European state-controlled energy company, reflecting both his institutional entrenchment and the Italian government's confidence in his leadership.

Descalzi's record, however, carries documented complexity. In 2014, Italian prosecutors opened a criminal investigation into Descalzi personally in connection with Eni's acquisition of OPL 245 — a prolific offshore Nigerian oil block — at a cost of approximately $1.3 billion. The deal involved Eni, Shell, and the Nigerian government, and was accompanied by persistent allegations that significant portions of the purchase price were funnelled through a shell company called Malabu Oil and Gas into private accounts. Descalzi was placed under investigation alongside the company. He consistently denied all allegations.

In March 2021, a Milan criminal court acquitted Descalzi, Eni, Shell, and all senior managers of all charges. The court found no case to answer. In 2022, Italian prosecutors dropped their appeal against the acquittal.

Eni, ICSID, and the Satellite Model

In November 2023, Eni filed an ICSID arbitration claim against Nigeria — not as a forum in which it necessarily expected to win, but as a coercive instrument to shift the negotiating posture of a sovereign state. The filing created financial exposure and reputational risk for Nigeria. Nigeria responded by dropping its own civil claim. Eni then suspended the arbitration. The outcome was reached without any public disclosure of its terms. Observers of international investment law describe this as a documented use of ICSID as a tactical pressure tool, not a genuine adjudication process.

The Satellite Model

In Eni's own description, SEARAH follows what it calls the "satellite model" — a strategy Eni has used in three prior major transactions: Vår Energi in Norway (2018), Azule Energy in Angola (2022, in partnership with BP), and Ithaca Energy in the United Kingdom (2024). In each case, Eni contributed assets into a separately incorporated vehicle, registered in a jurisdiction outside the host country, governed by the law of the incorporation country rather than the law of the host state. The consistent structural logic is identical: isolate the assets from host-country sovereign risk by placing them inside a corporate structure that cannot be directly reached by the host government's regulatory actions.

"The assets included in the transaction retain their current operating set-up," Eni noted in its March 2026 description of SEARAH. This is, at one level, a reassurance — the fields will still be operated by the same technical teams. But it is also a description of what a satellite vehicle does: it holds title at the corporate level while the operating entity continues to run day-to-day operations. The question of who owns the asset, and under what law, is settled at the holding-company level. The operating company simply manages the production.

The Legal Architecture — What We Know and What We Don't

The SEARAH legal architecture — UK incorporation, English law, UK-registered holding company — creates significant jurisdictional complexity. According to the UNCTAD BIT database (verified April 2026), no Bilateral Investment Treaty between Malaysia and Italy has been confirmed to exist. There is also no active BIT between Malaysia and the United Kingdom — the previous UK-Malaysia BIT lapsed, and no replacement is in force.

⚠ KEY FINDING: The UNCTAD BIT database (April 2026) confirms: No Italy-Malaysia BIT. No active UK-Malaysia BIT. This means the structure relies on English corporate law and UK courts — not an investment treaty framework. The question of what international protections Eni actually has, and what recourse Malaysia has, is more legally ambiguous than a BIT-protected structure would imply.

This means the legal protections available under this structure are governed by English contract and company law — not by any confirmed bilateral investment treaty. The JV agreement's terms govern the relationship between the parties. English courts have jurisdiction over disputes. This is a commercially legitimate structure. But the international treaty layer — the thing that typically makes investment agreements enforceable at the international level against sovereign governments — does not appear to exist in this case.

The question of what remedies Malaysia has, and what obligations Eni faces, is governed by the JV agreement and English law. That is the practical legal reality of this structure.

The Structural Finding

SEARAH is not accidentally registered in London. The UK structure is not a technicality. It is the point. The question is not whether the structure is illegal. The question is whether a sovereign resource of a nation was placed inside a legal architecture designed, at least in part, to make that sovereign resource harder for that nation to regulate.

If PETROS prevails in its challenge, and if the Malaysian government is then unable to simply direct how SEARAH's assets are to be managed because those assets sit inside a UK company governed by English law, the question becomes: what does Malaysia do?

It cannot unilaterally expropriate the UK company's assets without engaging UK legal processes. It cannot simply override the shareholder agreement without potential litigation exposure in English courts. And it cannot do any of this without incurring the risk of a commercial dispute resolved under English law in London — which is a fundamentally different position than if the assets were held inside a Malaysian-incorporated entity subject to Malaysian law.

PETRONAS has every right to enter into commercial partnerships. Eni has every right to structure those partnerships in ways that serve its commercial interests. The SEARAH legal architecture is commercially legitimate. But legitimacy and accountability are not the same thing.

A national resource belongs to a nation. When that resource is placed inside a corporate structure that is governed by a foreign law, owned in part by a foreign company, and registered in a foreign jurisdiction — at a moment when domestic courts are actively deciding who controls that resource — the people of that nation are entitled to know why. They are entitled to know who decided. They are entitled to know who approved it. They are entitled to know what their government understood it was agreeing to.

"The facts are not disputed. The company exists. The assets are real. The timeline is a matter of public record. What is missing is the explanation — and until that explanation is given, the question of whether Malaysia's petroleum future is being properly managed remains one that every Malaysian has the right to ask."

The Unanswered
What Nobody in Putrajaya Will Explain

The transaction has not yet closed. Eni's own statements, as recently as March 2026, indicate that "customary and governmental approvals" in both Malaysia and Indonesia were still being obtained. The deal was expected to reach closing in 2026, with operations beginning mid-2026. This means the conditions precedent have not all been satisfied. It means the Malaysian government — at some level — is still being asked to approve or acquiesce to a structure whose corporate form is already in existence. This is not a closed question. There is still time.

The PETRONAS-PETROS Question Is Not Academic

The Federal Court's decision to allow the PETRONAS constitutional challenge on March 16, 2026 is significant not because it resolves the dispute — it does not — but because it means the court has agreed the question is serious enough to hear.

The core issue is whether the Petroleum Development Act 1974, and the federal petroleum regime it established, validly applies in Sarawak, or whether Sarawak's own petroleum laws — passed by the Sarawak State Assembly and grounded in the Malaysia Agreement 1963 — take precedence. If PETROS prevails, the implications for PETRONAS's operating authority in Sarawak are substantial. If PETRONAS prevails, the PETROS framework is significantly weakened.

Neither outcome will need to be implemented through the actual fields — through the wells, the platforms, the pipelines, and the gas that flows to Bintulu. Those assets are now, in their corporate holding structure, partly inside SEARAH Limited — a UK company governed by English law.

The PETRONAS-PETROS dispute is not academic. SEARAH is not academic. The RM70 billion question is not academic. Every Malaysian who uses fuel, who pays electricity bills, who heats a home — has a stake in the answer.
